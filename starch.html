Starch



Starch processing is one of the oldest areas for the application of enzymes. For instance, dextrose, high fructose corn syrups are made using enzymes. They act as a substitute for ordinary sugar in food and beverage processing. KLSs’ future work in starch processing will focus on optimizing processes by developing improved enzymes.

Starch is the commonest storage carbohydrate in plants. It is used by the plants themselves, by microbes and by higher organisms so there is a great diversity of enzymes able to catalyse its hydrolysis. Starch from all plant sources occurs in the form of granules which differ markedly in size and physical characteristics from species to species. Generally, starch is boiled with acids and it gives glucose but this process contain has several drawbacks. It is now replace with enzymatic hydrolysis.

The dextrose equivalent value is indicates the percentage of hydrolysis of glycosidic linkages. Starch has a DE of effectively zero and glucose has a DE of 100.


There are three stages in the conversion of starch:

  1. gelatinisation, involving the dissolution of the nanogram-sized starch granules to form a viscous suspension.
  2. liquefaction, involving the partial hydrolysis of the starch, with concomitant loss in viscosity
  3. saccharification, involving the production of glucose and maltose by further hydrolysis

Starch liquefaction

Starch liquefaction using a-amylases but the principles are the same. Granular starch is slurried at 30-40% (w/w) with cold water after adjustment with pH, Ca2+ ions is added into medium (which stabilises and activates the enzyme) and the enzyme is added. When Termamyl is used, the slurry of starch plus enzyme is pumped continuously through a jet cooker, which is heated to 105 ºC using live steam. Gelatinisation occurs very rapidly and the enzymic activity, combined with the significant shear forces, begins the hydrolysis. The residence time in the jet cooker is very brief. The partly gelatinised starch is passed into a series of holding tubes maintained at 100-105 ºC and held for 5-8 min to complete the gelatinisation process. Hydrolysis to the required DE is completed in holding tanks for 1 to 2 h at the same temperature.


Starch Saccharification

Saccharification is the second process of starch hydrolysis. Depending upon the final product, further process is carried out with glucoamylase and fungal alpha amylase to break maltodextrins. After processed with glucoamylase, starch is converted into maltose and isomaltose. Fungal alpha amylase can also used to make high maltose containing syrup which gives relative high sweetness and increased fermentation.
